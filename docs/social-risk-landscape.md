# Social Risk Landscape

This document summarizes high-level public-interest risks that AI Police could help route, document, and mitigate in a rights-preserving, non-operational way.

It does not recommend autonomous enforcement. It is intended to guide support-first, referral-first, and audit-first system design.

## Scope

The focus areas in this brief are:

- India
- United States
- Conflict-affected and humanitarian settings
- Women and girls
- Children and adolescents

## Cross-Cutting Global Issues

Across countries and contexts, the same harm patterns recur:

- technology-facilitated abuse against women and girls,
- cyberbullying and harassment affecting children and adolescents,
- low reporting rates and weak access to formal support,
- fragmented response across schools, platforms, health systems, and public agencies,
- heightened risk in conflict, displacement, and humanitarian disruption.

### Evidence Highlights

- WHO states that about 1 in 3 women globally have experienced physical and/or sexual intimate partner violence or non-partner sexual violence in their lifetime, and that violence against women is both a public health problem and a human rights violation.
- WHO also notes that violence against women harms children directly and indirectly through emotional distress, morbidity, and family instability.
- UN Women reports that online and digital spaces have become sites of harassment, abuse, coercive control, stalking, and threats, while formal reporting remains low in many settings.
- UN Women also reports that women and girls in conflict-affected, climate-vulnerable, and humanitarian settings face disproportionately high risks of violence.

## India: Public-Interest Need Areas

India has a strong public-interest case for support systems that improve lawful digital reporting, triage, and referral for women and children.

### India Gaps and Pressures

- Large-scale digital participation means online harm can spread quickly across languages and platforms.
- Women and children need accessible pathways to report cyber abuse, exploitation, stalking, fraud-adjacent harassment, and image-based harm.
- Public systems must coordinate better across reporting, case routing, escalation, and follow-up.

### Institutional Context

The Ministry of Home Affairs describes the Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C) as a national nodal mechanism for cybercrime response. It includes:

- the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal,
- a stated special focus on cybercrime against women and children,
- threat analytics,
- training, research, and coordination functions,
- routing of complaints to State and Union Territory authorities.

### Example-Oriented Public Benefits for India

Safe, non-operational uses of AI Police in the Indian context could include:

- multilingual complaint intake support for women and families who do not know the correct reporting path,
- guided triage that separates urgent safety concerns from lower-severity digital nuisance reports,
- structured case summaries for nodal officers without replacing legal review,
- child-safety referral prompts for schools, guardians, and welfare agencies,
- public-awareness guidance that explains evidence preservation, reporting channels, and follow-up expectations.

### Women and Girls in India

A support-first AI layer could help reduce friction in:

- reporting harassment and intimidation,
- routing survivors to appropriate services,
- reducing repeated retelling of traumatic incidents through structured intake,
- surfacing high-risk patterns for human review.

### Children in India

A child-centered AI support layer could help with:

- parent and caregiver guidance,
- school referral workflows,
- age-appropriate risk escalation,
- clearer separation between welfare response and law-enforcement referral.

## United States: Public-Interest Need Areas

The United States has strong public evidence for youth online harm, cyberbullying reporting gaps, and the need for cross-sector support models that combine schools, families, public health, and platform reporting.

### United States Gaps and Pressures

- The U.S. Surgeon General notes that youth social media use is nearly universal and that harms are meaningful even where benefits exist.
- The same advisory states that children and adolescents who spend more than 3 hours a day on social media face double the risk of mental health problems including depression and anxiety symptoms.
- The advisory also states that current evidence is insufficient to conclude that social media is safe for children and adolescents.
- Federal guidance on cyberbullying emphasizes documentation, reporting, school coordination, and escalation to law enforcement only when criminal thresholds are met.

### Example-Oriented Public Benefits for the United States

Safe uses of AI Police in the U.S. context could include:

- family-facing guidance for documenting and reporting cyberbullying,
- school support workflows that help distinguish discipline issues from safety emergencies,
- support-first routing for teen girls experiencing online harassment, coercion, or threats,
- referral guidance to public-health, school, and child-safety resources before punitive escalation,
- transparency tools that help agencies explain when an issue belongs with schools, platforms, social services, or law enforcement.

### Women and Girls in the United States

A safe support layer could assist with:

- triaging online stalking, coercive control, or abuse complaints,
- generating plain-language reporting guidance,
- identifying when a complaint should move to survivor support services,
- reducing administrative burden on public helplines and intake desks.

### Children and Adolescents in the United States

A public-interest AI layer could help with:

- cyberbullying evidence intake,
- school notification support,
- youth mental-health-aware escalation guidance,
- child online exploitation referral prompts tied to existing reporting channels.

## Conflict-Affected and Humanitarian Settings

Conflict-affected settings require the most restrictive and rights-protective deployment posture.

### Current Gaps and Pressures

- UN Women reports that women in humanitarian contexts face severe risks of gender-based violence.
- Conflict, displacement, economic breakdown, and service disruption intensify exposure to abuse while reducing access to formal help.
- Children face compounded risks where schools, health systems, and family support networks are disrupted.

### Safe Framing for Conflict-Affected Use

In conflict-affected settings, AI Police should not be framed as a coercive or intelligence tool.

It should be framed only as:

- a civilian safety and referral support layer,
- a case documentation assistant,
- a service triage assistant,
- an accountability and audit support layer for humanitarian actors or lawful civilian authorities.

### Example-Oriented Benefits for Women and Girls

- survivor-centered intake support,
- referral guidance to local protection or health services,
- structured escalation for urgent risk without exposing survivors to repeated questioning,
- safer audit records for service coordination.

### Example-Oriented Benefits for Children

- child-safe intake for protection concerns,
- referral-first handling rather than punitive routing,
- structured guidance for guardians, schools, shelters, and support providers,
- risk flags for interrupted schooling, displacement-related vulnerability, and exploitation indicators.

## Key Design Implications

The research points to five design implications for this repository:

- support-first workflows are more appropriate than punishment-first workflows,
- reporting and referral friction is a core public-interest problem,
- women and children need specialized safety pathways,
- jurisdiction-specific safeguards are mandatory,
- conflict-affected contexts require the highest restraint and strongest human oversight.
